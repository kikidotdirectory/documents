*to sappy*

i found the first part of *The Stranger* to be quite slow and depressing. it was at this point when i had messaged you, saying it might take me longer than the last to finish. there was something so apathetic, so complacent, so detached, that made me so uncomfortable. in some ways, it felt like something that was staring at me.

as the book went on though, i found myself more understanding and in some ways, admirable. his ability to look past the stigmas of the world, albeit in ways that i disagree with, is something that stuck out to me. to me, the stranger was a book on conformism. when he is being tried, the court became one on how much they saw him fit to be one of them rather than his guilt in the crime. 

i wonder what time period *The Stranger* was set in. it feels like the society is supposed to feel draconian and the reader is expected to contrast that society with the one that they live in.

the extent of which the narrator is different from society makes them incredibly effective at conveying the feeling of being othered. i found incredible sympathy in his inability to understand the expectations others had of him, something that made his conflict with the chaplain even more compelling. 

> In his view a man's justice was a vain thing; only God's justice mattered. I pointed out that the former had condemned me.

i think what Camus' intent is— and what i found he was successful in doing— is making me extremely conscious of the times that i have acted like the narrator. while most people don't act in apathy as serially as he did, they still have moments where they do not act in ways they wish they did in which the narrator's sudden end leaves a sense of discomfort.

what if he spent more time with marie? what if he had kissed her more than once since early morning? what if he had or had not done many things?

it also makes me question how i judge others. while Camus makes no judgment of the ability of people to judge one of their peers, he does make plainly clear the consequences. i find that beautiful in the book. it paints a story in extreme circumstances, someone not "normal" yet apart of society. yes, he is complicit in something disgusting, but should that influence his sentencing in something unrelated? it reinforces the importance of an unbiased jury.

i think those are the sentiments that linger with me: it makes me want to care more, it makes me want to understand others more. it makes me more passionate on not being apathetic when the world feels like it's crashing down.

from i literary standpoint, one of the open questions i have is that of the heat. while it references the physical location, it seems there's something about the heat in particular that denotes something of importance that i'm still pondering.
